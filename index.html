Coronavirus analysis and comparison with other pandemics

Main Idea

Our main idea is to analyze the new Coronavirus and the H1N1, Ebola and SARS 2003 pandemics. We want to perform exploratory analysis on these three pandemics in order to identify common patterns with Coronavirus. In order to do so, we will find the distribution of cases and deaths for these pandemics, see which top 10 countries were mostly affected and how(cases and deaths over pandemic duration) and finally their geographical spread. From this procedure we will understand if there are patterns between Coronavirus and the other diseases. Moreover, we also want to analyze Coronavirus in different countries with reference to their population, health facilities, and finally we use machine learning and try to forecast its spread regarding the number of cases and deaths.

Interest

Our interest has come from the wide spread of Coronavirus globally and how it has affected our everyday life. Among the pandemics of the 21st century, that's the one which has changed our way of living comparing to the others. So, we think that finding any common point with the other pandemics might help us figure out how to confront it and return to the way we lived.

Reproduction Rate

To dive deeper into the coronavirus and how it has spread in selected countries, we decided to calculate and plot the temporal evolution of reproduction rate.

The reproduction rate is calculated as a smoothed moving average over a period of four days. These numbers indicate how well countries are handling the spread of the virus in terms of countermeasures, i.e. enforcing social distancing, home quarantines, locking down parts of society.


A reproduction rate of 0.0 means no new infections, whereas a reproduction rate of 1.0 means that every sick person infects 1 other person.


  • Top20 - 1st half
  • Top20 - 2nd half
  • Nordic
  • Team

In the first graph especially Italy stands out, having a global max reproduction rate of 2.46 at 20 days after the first confirmed case. This massive peak explains why Italy's healthcare system has been under such immense pressure. Russia and France peak around the same time, at day 30 after the first confirmed case, and their temporal reproduction rate follow a similar evolution from approximately day 25. Similarly, India peaks around day 30 but have a much more drastic increase and decrease in reproduction rate. This indicates a quick spread of the virus but also a quite efficient confinement strategy in order to decrease the reproduction rate

For the second half of the top20 countries Belgium, Spain and South Korea all peak in the third week of infections, but interestingly enough both South Korea and Belgium manage to enforce measures to lower the reproduction rate drastically again after the peak, whereas Spain does not have the same success. This results in the slow decrease in reproduction rate for Spain. South Korea have experience from a similar situation during the SARS pandemic and Belgium is a much smaller country, which might explain why they are more successful in their countermeasures. Another explanation could simply be that Spain did not act in time, and did not manage to enforce proper countermeasures to stop the virus from spreading.

Lastly, notice the exponentially decreasing nature that all reproduction rates follow. This is due to the infection slowing down and is shown this way due to the smoothed moving average.
