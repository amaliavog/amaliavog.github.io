Coronavirus analysis and comparison with other pandemics

Main Idea

Our main idea is to analyze the new Coronavirus and the H1N1, Ebola and SARS 2003 pandemics. We want to perform exploratory analysis on these three pandemics in order to identify common patterns with Coronavirus. In order to do so, we will find the distribution of cases and deaths for these pandemics, see which top 10 countries were mostly affected and how(cases and deaths over pandemic duration) and finally their geographical spread. From this procedure we will understand if there are patterns between Coronavirus and the other diseases. Moreover, we also want to analyze Coronavirus in different countries with reference to their population, health facilities, and finally we use machine learning and try to forecast its spread regarding the number of cases and deaths.

Interest

Our interest has come from the wide spread of Coronavirus globally and how it has affected our everyday life. Among the pandemics of the 21st century, that's the one which has changed our way of living comparing to the others. So, we think that finding any common point with the other pandemics might help us figure out how to confront it and return to the way we lived.

Reproduction Rate

To dive deeper into the coronavirus and how it has spread in selected countries, we decided to calculate and plot the temporal evolution of reproduction rate.

The reproduction rate is calculated as a smoothed moving average over a period of four days. These numbers indicate how well countries are handling the spread of the virus in terms of countermeasures, i.e. enforcing social distancing, home quarantines, locking down parts of society.


A reproduction rate of 0.0 means no new infections, whereas a reproduction rate of 1.0 means that every sick person infects 1 other person.


  • Top20 - 1st half
  • Top20 - 2nd half
  • Nordic
  • Team

In the first graph especially Italy stands out, having a global max reproduction rate of 2.46 at 20 days after the first confirmed case. This massive peak explains why Italy's healthcare system has been under such immense pressure. Russia and France peak around the same time, at day 30 after the first confirmed case, and their temporal reproduction rate follow a similar evolution from approximately day 25. Similarly, India peaks around day 30 but have a much more drastic increase and decrease in reproduction rate. This indicates a quick spread of the virus but also a quite efficient confinement strategy in order to decrease the reproduction rate

For the second half of the top20 countries Belgium, Spain and South Korea all peak in the third week of infections, but interestingly enough both South Korea and Belgium manage to enforce measures to lower the reproduction rate drastically again after the peak, whereas Spain does not have the same success. This results in the slow decrease in reproduction rate for Spain. South Korea have experience from a similar situation during the SARS pandemic and Belgium is a much smaller country, which might explain why they are more successful in their countermeasures. Another explanation could simply be that Spain did not act in time, and did not manage to enforce proper countermeasures to stop the virus from spreading.

Lastly, notice the exponentially decreasing nature that all reproduction rates follow. This is due to the infection slowing down and is shown this way due to the smoothed moving average.

Machine Learning

Throughout the project, we have analyzed current situation of COVID-19 and compared it with other pandemics. Now, we will build machine learning models, to predict number of cases in Denmark, Greece, and Turkey. We have gathered different kinds of parameters which would be affective to predict number of cases. For each country, we have the features geographical features which are latitude and longitude values. We have COVID-19 related statistical measures which are number of confirmed cases, number of deaths and number of recovered people on each day. We downloaded weather related parameters such as temperature, precipitation and more for each day. We have applied some feature engineering steps to create meaningful dataset from aforementioned features. We have decided to apply Random Forest, XGBoosting, Linear Regression, Ridge Regression and Lasso Regression machine learning algorithms to predict number of confirmed cases for each day. As a result of our implementations, best model is ‘Ridge Regression’ with ‘Regularization Factor’ (alpha) equals to 50. This model results roughly predicting averagely 425 people more or less.

As a second step applying machine learning application we thought of adding more features in order to predict better. Number of hospital beds, medical doctors, nurses and midwives are important metrics to fight against the virus. Especially number of hospital beds is an important metric in terms of capacity for people who can be treated at the same time. Therefore we think that it may have a correlation with mortality ratio since distressingly some of the countries faced with capacity exceed on hospital beds. Fighting with a pandemic is a very expensive expense for the economies as well. Therefore, to scale this cost for each country, GDP and population are important features to be added as well.

United Nations Human Development Programme publishes Human Development Results each year and Human Development Index which is abbreviated as HDI is calculated based on specific parameters. HDI’s components are 'Life expectancy at birth', 'Expected years of schooling', 'Mean years of schooling', 'Gross national income (GNI) per capita' are added to dataframe. This index and components are used to classify countries. Parameters related to education has an effect on people's reactions on country policies. For example, educated people are more likely to obey the rules under lockdown. Life expectancy is related with the health conditions of work industries and development level of healthcare system. After adding these features to the dataset, again, best model is ‘Ridge Regression’ with ‘Regularization Factor’ (alpha) equals to 50.

This model results roughly predicting averagely 398 people more or less which is predicting 27 cases better than previous model. These predictions are not good enough. Because predicting 398 people more or less on a daily base is a huge error. Therefore, we can conclude that our predictions are weak. Because we skip so much important parameters that we could not feed into our dataset. For example, there are certain number of infected people which aren't detected with COVID-19 but they spread this virus. Because some of people does not show symptoms but they are disease carriers. There some people who aren't aware of the fact that they show COVID-19 symptoms and spread virus. And there are young people who does not care about spreading the virus and behave carelessly and causing people to be infected and pass away. Each country takes some different precautions, but we could not objectify them as numbers to add to the model. And there is incubation period of this virus as well. People may be infected with this virus and won't show any symptoms, but they can spread this virus during incubation period of virus. People's immune system differs that we could not objectify as number to our model as well. As a sum up, these kinds of aspects increase or decrease the spread of virus and there are more of other events which are hard to objectify and add as a feature to the model. Therefore, our model isn't working good to count it as a trustworthy model.

Discussion

As we can see from our analysis, it’s not possible to compare Coronavirus with the other pandemics, since the duration and the evolve of those four pandemics virus very different. The only thing we can expect is to follow the same route as SARS where the cases and deaths stabilized eventually and the virus was eliminated. Furthermore, we see that Coronavirus has shown the more expansion among the countries, while Ebola has mostly affected Africa and SARS 2003 Eastern Asia but not with the intension as Coronavirus. Another important point, is that the new Coronavirus has the most deaths comparing the other pandemics. So, as we can see weather or economy might also affect how the pandemic will evolve. Finally, trying to predict the number of new cases in Denmark, Turkey and Greece didn’t have good results since as said above there are too many factors affecting it. However, we would like to further improve our project by experimenting with other attributes and make more accurate about the number of cases and deaths in these countries or in even more.
